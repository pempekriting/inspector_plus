# Final Flag

A flag that occurs at the end of a trend. Every reversal follows some kind of flag, making final flag reversals common. Understanding what makes a flag likely to be the last one before a trend reverses is key to anticipating and trading reversals.

## Characteristics

1. **Trend Duration**: The flag occurs in a trend that has gone on for dozens of bars
2. **Profit Taking**: Trend traders may begin taking profits
3. **Countertrend Aggression**: Countertrend traders become more aggressive
4. **Weaker Pullback**: The pullback from the flag is usually weak and often has bear bars and tails indicating bulls are weak

## Why Final Flags Work

Most new highs in bull trends find profit takers instead of new buyers. Scalpers take profits at and just above the prior high. This results in most new highs being followed by pullbacks instead of extended breakouts.

However, since most reversal attempts fail, most new highs do not result in reversals. The reversal often looks strong but is usually followed by just another bull flag.

## Trading Final Flags

- **Sell Setup**: When a bull trend ends with a final flag, look for sell setups
- **Wait for Confirmation**: Do not simply sell because there is a flag at the end of a trend - wait for the reversal signal bar
- **Two-Legged Move Expected**: Final flag reversals usually lead to at least two-legged moves

## Final Flag vs First Flag

A first flag in a trend is a continuation pattern (bull flag, bear flag). A final flag precedes a reversal. The key difference is position in the trend and the context of the move.

## Example Pattern

Bar 1 ended two legs down from the open, broke out of a sideways bear flag after a protracted move. This could be a final flag leading to a reversal up. The reversal bar triggered the final flag long, expecting at least two legs up.
