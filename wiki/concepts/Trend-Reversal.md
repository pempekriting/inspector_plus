# Trend Reversal

A change from a bull trend to a bear trend, or from a bear trend to a bull trend. Also includes change from trending behavior to trading range behavior (and vice versa), since trading range is opposite to trending.

## Characteristics

- **Reversal Bar**: Trend bar in opposite direction of the trend. Bull reversal bar has tail at bottom, close above open near top. Bear reversal bar has tail at top, close below open near bottom.
- **Two-Legged Move**: Most reversals lead to at least two-legged moves in the opposite direction.
- **Test of Old Extreme**: Markets often reverse after a test of the trend's extreme (high or low).

## Reversal Types

### Spike Reversal (Climactic)

V-shaped reversal where a spike in one direction is followed by a spike in the opposite direction. Simply a breakout and then a failed breakout.

### Wedge Reversal

Three-push move with each push lower (for top) or higher (for bottom). First target is the bottom of the wedge pattern. Next target is measured move based on pattern height.

### Final Flag Reversal

Reversal that follows a flag at the end of a trend. Every reversal follows some kind of flag.

### Double Top/Bottom Reversal

Reversal from a traditional or micro double top or bottom pattern.

## Key Rules

1. **Most Reversals Fail**: About 80% of reversal attempts fail. Only take reversal trades when signs of strength are present.

2. **Wait for Always-In Flip**: Do not take reversal trades until the always-in direction changes.

3. **Look for Signs of Strength**:
   - Strong reversal bar with large body and small tails
   - Next 2-3 bars have bull/bear bodies at least average size
   - Spike grows without pullback for 5-10 bars
   - Prior trend line breaks
   - Pullback to test old high/low lacks momentum

4. **Micro Double Before Reversal**: Most tradable reversals begin with at least a micro double bottom/top, traditional double, or final flag.

5. **Trend Line Break Not Enough**: A break of a trend line alone is not enough to look for shorts. The break must be strong before traders believe the move will sustain.

## Reversal vs Continuation

Not every reversal attempt succeeds. When a trend resumes after a pullback, it is a continuation, not a failed reversal. A failed reversal becomes a breakout pullback in the direction of the original trend.
