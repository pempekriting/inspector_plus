# Wedge Pattern

A three-push move where each swing high is lower (wedge top) or each swing low is higher (wedge bottom). Also called "three pushes" - the first target is the beginning of the pattern, and the next target is a measured move based on the height of the pattern.

## Wedge Top (Bear Reversal)

Three pushes up where each push fails to exceed the prior high. Creates a narrowing pattern like a rising wedge. Usually leads to at least two legs down.

**Characteristics:**
- Each successive push up has less momentum
- Often has trend channel line overshoot on third push
- Third push often forms a bear reversal bar

**Trading:**
- Short when price breaks below the reversal bar
- First target: bottom of the wedge (lowest point of the pullback from first push)
- Second target: measured move equal to height of wedge

## Wedge Bottom (Bull Reversal)

Three pushes down where each push fails to go below the prior low. Creates a narrowing pattern like a falling wedge.

**Characteristics:**
- Each successive push down has less momentum
- Third push often forms a bull reversal bar
- Often followed by at least two legs up

## Wedge Bear Flag

A wedge in the direction of the trend that acts as a pullback in an ongoing trend. For example, a wedge bear flag in a bull trend is a three-push move up that pulls back within the larger bull trend.

## Wedge vs Low 3

Low 3 is essentially a wedge bear flag variant - a third lower low in a bear correction. The terms are sometimes used interchangeably, but wedge typically implies a more distinct three-push pattern with clear channel boundaries.

## Key Rules

1. **Not a Buy Signal in Tight Channel**: A wedge bottom is not a buy signal when in a tight bear channel. Wait for breakout of the channel before buying.

2. **Parabolic Move Warning**: If the trend channel line is steeper than prior ones, the move may be parabolic and likely to reverse.

3. **Fourth Bar Rejection**: If the bar after the third push mostly overlaps the prior bar, it indicates acceptance of the price rather than rejection - reversal less likely.

4. **Wait for Second Entry**: Do not buy the first breakout attempt of a tight channel. Wait for a second entry.

## Examples from AL Brooks

- Wedge tops lead to two legs down
- Wedge bottoms often lead to at least two-legged rally
- Failed wedge often falls for a measured move
- Shrinking stairs (each breakout extends less than prior) signal waning momentum
