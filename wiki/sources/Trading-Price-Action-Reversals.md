# Trading Price Action Reversals

## Source Summary

AL Brooks' *Trading Price Action Reversals* is the second book in a three-volume series on price action trading. The book focuses on how to identify and trade reversal patterns in financial markets, particularly in futures, Emini, and stocks. The core thesis: the market is always trying to break out, and then tries to make every breakout fail. Success comes from assessing whether a breakout will succeed or fail.

## Key Themes

1. **Reversal Trading**: Every trend reversal follows recognizable patterns. Reversal trades often have 50% or lower probability but offer reward-to-risk ratios of 2:1 or better.

2. **Signs of Strength**: Strong reversals share characteristics with any strong move - strong reversal bars, consecutive follow-through bars, spikes without pullbacks, and breaks of prior trend lines.

3. **Climactic Reversals**: V-shaped reversals (spike followed by spike in opposite direction) are simply a breakout and then a failed breakout.

4. **Three-Push Patterns**: Wedges and similar patterns create three pushes in one direction before reversal. Target is the beginning of the pattern, then a measured move based on pattern height.

5. **Final Flags**: Every reversal follows some kind of flag. Understanding what makes a flag likely to be the last one before a trend reverses is key to anticipating and trading reversals.

6. **Always-In Direction**: Traders should constantly assess whether the market is trending. The always-in approach helps make that determination - if always-in long in a bull trend, only look for longs or wait for always-in flip.

## Trading Concepts

### Bar Counting Basics

- **High 1**: First bar with a high above the prior bar in a bull flag or near bottom of trading range
- **High 2**: Second consecutive bar with a higher high (third is High 3, etc.)
- **Low 1**: First bar with a low below the prior bar in a bear flag or near top of trading range
- **Low 2**: Second consecutive bar with a lower low (third is Low 3, etc.)

### Reversal Bar Characteristics

A bull reversal bar in a bear leg typically has:
- Tail at the bottom
- Close above open and near the top
- Large bull trend body

A bear reversal bar in a bull leg has:
- Tail at the top
- Close below open and near the bottom

### Wedge Reversal

Three-push move where each push is lower than the prior. First target is the bottom of the wedge (beginning of pattern). Next target is a measured move based on height of the pattern.

### Expanding Triangle

At least five swings (sometimes seven, rarely nine), each greater than the prior. Traps traders on each new breakout. In a bullish reversal (expanding triangle bottom), it has enough strength to rally above the last higher high, trapping longs, then collapses to a third low.

### Final Flag Reversal

A flag that occurs at the end of a trend. Characteristics:
- Flag occurs in a trend that has gone on for dozens of bars
- Trend traders may begin taking profits
- Countertrend traders become more aggressive

## Trading Rules

1. **Most Reversals Fail**: About 80% of reversal attempts fail. Institutions constantly try to reverse trends but fail most of the time.

2. **Wait for Always-In Direction Change**: Do not take reversal trades until the always-in direction flips. If in a bull trend, only trade longs until evidence shows the direction has changed.

3. **Two-Legged Pullback**: Most reversals lead to at least two-legged moves. Wait for the pullback to establish the first leg before expecting reversal.

4. **Test Before Reversal**: Spike pullbacks (in direction of trend) do not need to get tested since traders agree the trend is resuming. Spike reversals (countertrend) usually get tested because traders are less willing to believe a reversal will succeed.

5. **Micro Double Bottoms/Tops**: Most tradable reversals begin with at least a micro double bottom or top, a traditional double bottom or top, or a final flag reversal.

6. **Trend Line Breaks Alone Not Enough**: A break of a trend line alone is not enough to look for shorts. The break has to be strong before traders believe the bulls can sustain a move up.

## Core Philosophy

> "The market is always trying to break out, and then the market tries to make every breakout fail. This is the most fundamental aspect of all trading and is at the heart of everything that we do."

The market is controlled by institutions running buy and sell programs. These generate most of the volume and create most of the trading opportunities. Individual traders should think of institutions as any entity that trades enough volume to be a significant contributor to price action.
